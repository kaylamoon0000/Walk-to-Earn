Proposal

AI Walk-to-Earn Harlem is a public-space activation platform designed to increase pedestrian movement, support small businesses, and improve the experience of Harlem Plaza. The project combines route personalization, walking verification, landmark checkpoints, and reward systems in one user-friendly experience.

Project Overview

The platform rewards users for walking along recommended plaza routes, reaching checkpoints near local businesses, and verifying those visits through QR-style interactions. Its goal is to transform the plaza from a pass-through space into an active destination where movement, public health, and local economic activity reinforce one another.

Problem Definition

Many plazas feel underused because they do not provide strong reasons to stay, explore, or interact with nearby businesses. Small businesses may sit next to foot traffic without truly benefiting from it. This platform addresses that gap by making walking visible, measurable, and rewarding.

Site / Context

The design responds to movement patterns, comfort zones, safety, accessibility, use patterns, and business activity. Direct paths become guided loops, underused corners become checkpoint destinations, and nearby storefronts become active parts of the pedestrian experience.

Final Redesign Proposal

The selected direction combines route guidance, checkpoint verification, and reward personalization. Users see a clear path, businesses get measurable participation data, and the plaza gains a stronger public identity through visible activity and destination-based walking.

Proposal

AI Walk-to-Earn Harlem is a public-space activation platform designed to increase pedestrian movement, support small businesses, and improve the experience around Montefiore Square. It combines route personalization, account-based progress, walking verification, checkpoints, and reward systems in one unified flow while also separating walker and business account experiences.

Project Overview

The platform rewards users for walking along recommended routes, reaching checkpoints near businesses, and verifying those visits through QR-style interactions. Business accounts gain a separate analytics environment focused on participation and performance instead of walker progression.

Problem Definition

Many public spaces do not create strong reasons to stay, explore, or support nearby stores. This platform responds by making movement visible, measurable, and directly tied to local business participation while giving each user type a role-specific interface.

Site / Context

The design responds to movement patterns, accessibility, comfort, safety, and business activity. Direct paths become guided routes, underused segments become destinations, and nearby storefronts become active parts of the public-space experience.

Final Proposal

The chosen direction combines route guidance, real business checkpoints, account-based saved progress, separate walker and business logins, reward personalization, and a trackable user journey. Walkers and businesses each see what is most relevant to them.
